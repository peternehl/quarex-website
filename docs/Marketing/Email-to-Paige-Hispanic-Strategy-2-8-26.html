Strategic Asset: Hispanic Studies + Multilingual Election Info

February 8, 2026 — For Paige Rucker Orme

Paige,

I want to flag something that emerged this week that I think has significant marketing potential — possibly worth building a campaign around.

The Differentiator: 13-Language Election Information

Election2026.net now provides candidate information in 13 languages: English, Spanish, Chinese (Simplified and Traditional), Vietnamese, Korean, Tagalog, Arabic, French, German, Russian, Hindi, Portuguese, and Japanese.

Who else does this? Nobody.

Ballotpedia is English-only. Vote Smart is English-only. The major news outlets cover elections in English, sometimes Spanish. We're offering genuine multilingual civic access at a scale that doesn't exist elsewhere.

The Trust-Building Entry Point: Hispanic Studies

This week we launched four complete curricula under the Hispanic Studies umbrella — 79 courses covering:

  • Hispanic Cultures & Identity in America
  • U.S.-Latin America Relations
  • The Hispanic American Experience Today
  • Hispanic Contributions to American Culture

The content originates in English but the AI delivers answers and generates follow-up questions in Spanish — making it genuinely accessible to Spanish-dominant learners and heritage speakers.

The Strategy: Respect First, Then Civic Engagement

Here's what I realized: you can't walk into a community and say "let me tell you who to vote for." You have to demonstrate respect first.

Hispanic Studies does that. It says: Your history matters. Your contributions are foundational to this country. We took the time to document it properly.

Once that credibility is established, the same platform can deliver honest, accessible election information — in Spanish or any of 12 other languages — and be trusted.

Marketing Angle

I see two possible approaches:

  • Lead with the unique: "The only election resource available in 13 languages" — emphasize civic accessibility and underserved communities
  • Lead with the cultural: Position Hispanic Studies as the entry point, with election info as a natural extension of our commitment to the community

Either way, the combination of deep cultural content + unprecedented multilingual election access is something we can own. It's not a feature — it's a positioning.
